About

I've been noticing things my whole life without knowing there was a name for it. These three stories are the most honest answer I have to why that name might be advertising.

In 8th standard, I borrowed money from a friend and bought a Cadbury Silk for a girl. I wasn't really thinking about chocolate.

I was following a script that Cadbury had already written. That in India, if you love someone, you give them a Silk. The ad had done its job so completely that I wasn't even aware it had done anything. I was just a boy with borrowed money, standing outside a classroom, holding purple packaging like it was a marriage proposal.

That's when I realised tapping the right emotion is the backbone of any narrative.

"Darr ke aage jeet hai" was my wallpaper throughout UPSC preparation. Not because I'm a Mountain Dew loyalist but because fear is the one emotion that nobody escapes. Gabbar Singh knew it. Field Marshal Manekshaw once said that anyone who claims he feels no fear is either lying or he's a Gorkha.

Mountain Dew just found a way to sell a drink through it. Someone sat in a room, picked fear out of the thousands of human emotions available, and built something that outlasted the campaign. Nike did it with laziness. Cadbury did it with love. The product is almost incidental.

While conducting field research for a Bangalore-based political consultancy during the 2024 Lok Sabha elections, I realised the same behaviour applies to voters. Choosing a candidate is not very different from picking a packet of chips off a rack. Every option has a USP, but only the one that taps the right emotion gets picked.

Still finding those connections, only this time where it belongs the most.

01 / MuscleBlaze
सनक
MuscleBlaze — Voiceover Concept

Sanak

For everyone who once burned for something and then slowly stopped.

Voiceover Fitness Hindi
The Insight
"Every MuscleBlaze campaign spoke to the person already on the path. Nobody had spoken to the person who left it."

After reading four campaigns, a gap became visible. Tum Nahi Samjhoge identified the tribe. Ziddi Hoon Main gave it clarity. Naam Hai Ziddi proved it. Answer the Call explained the origin.

But every person in every film was already Ziddi. What about the person who used to be Ziddi and stopped? The deviation from the path is the untouched territory. Sanak lives there.

The word itself is the insight. Sanak means an obsession so complete it becomes identity. Everyone was sanki once. The voiceover does not motivate. It asks you to stop running from what you already know about yourself.

Brand Reading First Emotional Recall
The Voiceover — Four Sections
I.
सनकी, सरफ़िरे, जुनूनी — कई नाम हैं इनके,
क्योंकि कमज़ोर कहलाना इन्हें मंज़ूर नहीं।
मंज़ूर भी कैसे हो
वो उम्र से बड़ा हौसला जो ठहरा
जो देता है सनक इन्हें रोज़ खुद से भिड़ जाने की,
खुद को तपाने की,
हर बाज़ी खुद को बेहतर बनाने की।
II.
कैसे हो महसूस और कैसे ही आए नज़र
बिना परखे, बिना झांके खुद के भीतर
वो शून्य का सन्नाटा, वो एकांत में पनपती सनक
जो कह रही है तुमसे —
वो ओढ़ अनगिनत घावों की ढालों को
और देख उन ललकारों भरी नज़रों से उन खौफ़ खाती चुनौतियों को
और चबा डालो उन लोहे की खुराकों को।
III.
इन मुश्किलों की आहट और सिसकियों की गर्माहट से
पूछने लगी हैं अब भीतर की उफनती लहरें
कब लगेगी वो खुद से होड़
जो देगी हर बंदिश को तोड़
तो उठ, बना रास्ता — अब खुद से नाता जोड़।
IV.
सूख चुका ये औसत दर्जे का जीवन,
लगा रहा बस एक गुहार — बरस अब मेरे अपने भीतर,
सुलगा दे अब एक ऐसी आग
कि टूट सके ये ठहरा सा दौर।
कर दे राख इन आधे-अधूरे ख़्वाबों के निशान,
या तो बनाऊँ मैं खुद की पहचान
या मिट जाऊँ लड़ते-लड़ते इस सनक के नाम।
01
The Gap Found
All four existing campaigns spoke to people already on the Ziddi path. The person who left the path and is now living an average life he once refused to accept — that person had never been addressed. That gap is what Sanak occupies.
02
Why It Does Not Motivate
Motivation assumes you need to be pushed toward something new. Sanak assumes you already know. It asks you to face what you have been avoiding. That is a harder and more honest ask, which is why it fits a brand that has always refused to be comfortable.
03
Bhag Mat
The closing line is not a tagline. It is the diagnosis. The average life is not a failure of capability. It is a failure of not running from the truth of what you once wanted. Accept kar. Train kar. The product is the physical proof of the mental decision.
भाग मत. Accept kar. Train kar.

02 / Zepto
Zepto — TVC Concept

Chugli Se
Bhi Tez.

Faster than the drama. Faster than the consequences.

TVC Family Comedy Quick Commerce
The Insight
"Every Indian family runs on two things: chaos and covering for each other. Zepto became the third player."

Zepto's existing ads used drama as the villain. A mother-in-law scolding. A boss speaking. An uncomfortable situation that Zepto interrupted. The zag here was different — the villain is not a person. It is a family conspiracy. The sister's chugli.

Speed here is not a feature. It is a peace-keeping device. Zepto does not just deliver in ten minutes. It delivers before the consequences of a mistake can land. That is a more specific and more human use of speed.

The football commentary running parallel to the domestic drama is the structural device that gives the script its rhythm. Each commentary beat mirrors a family beat. The doorbell rings at the exact moment of the final shot.

Full Script
OPEN
Drawing room. Afternoon. Mother on the sofa, adding groceries to Zepto. Sister watching a movie. Brother walks in, snatches the remote, switches to football. His team is about to score. Sister is agitated. He ignores her.
BEAT 1
Father walks in from the bedroom. "Mera black bag nahi mil raha. Mujhe packing karni hai, shaam ko jaana hai." Son freezes. Looks at mother, jaw clenched. Mother realises and frowns. Sister clocks everything.
BEAT 2
"Tu trip pe bag bhool gaya na?" Sister jumps up. Commentator: Opposition has taken the ball. A roar from the crowd. Brother grabs sister. They wrestle on the sofa, slide to the floor, crawling toward father's room.
BEAT 3
Mother quietly opens Zepto. Finds the bag. Types in instructions: remove all tags. Commentator: Strong defence holding the line. Siblings almost at father's door. Striker at the final defence, all set for the shot.
RESOLVE
Doorbell rings. Mother walks to the door, bag in hand, just as father steps out of his room. Commentator: A close call, a missed opportunity. She hands him the bag. "Kaun tha darwaze pe?" "Iska dost tha." And that is a penalty. Father turns to son still on the floor. "Saara din dosti nibhao aur match dekho. Bahut badhiya hai." Takes the bag. Walks away. Sister, still on the floor, laughs.
01
Why This Family
Every Indian viewer has lived a version of this scene. The comedy is recognition, not invention. The mother-as-fixer is a cultural truth that needs no setup. The brother's guilt, the sister's opportunism, the father's obliviousness — all instantly readable.
02
The Commentary Device
The football commentary running parallel to the domestic drama gives the script a rhythm that earns every laugh without spelling it out. The doorbell ringing at the moment of the final shot is the creative peak. Two timelines, one resolution.
03
Where Zepto Appears
Zepto does not appear until the doorbell. It solves the problem silently. The tagline lands because it has just been demonstrated, not promised. Chugli se bhi tez is not a claim. It is the conclusion the audience just reached themselves.
Chugli se bhi tez.

03 / Tic Tac
Tic Tac — TVC Concept

Musical
Chair

A bored uncle on the metro. A voiceover that won't let him scroll.

TVC Confectionery Role Reversal
The Insight
"Every Tic Tac ad targets youth. The uncle voice tells youth what to do. This one turns the table."

Tic Tac's category is youth and cool. The conventional move is to show young people doing something fun. Every Centre Fresh, every Mentos, every competitor does the same thing — the voiceover is preachy, the character is young, the product is the reward for being cool.

The zag: uncles in Indian society are the ones who preach. They tell you to put the phone down. They scold you for brain rot. But fathers and uncles are now the ones most stuck in the scroll loop. The irony is visible and nobody has used it.

Putting a 50-something uncle with a moustache in a metro full of young people, and having a young voiceover preach to him, inverts every convention of the category. The uncle savouring two Tic Tacs with his eyes closed in slow motion is the visual that makes him briefly, genuinely cool. Not despite his age. Because of it.

The Script
OPEN
Metro. An uncle, big moustache, sitting. Bored. Sees others watching reels. Pulls out his smartphone to do the same.
VOICEOVER
Bore ho rahe ho uncle? Mann nahi lag raha? Magar ye paap nahi karne dengey hum aapko.
Simple hai uncle.
Tic Tac ko kholo.
2 goli nikaalo.
1 jeeb k upar, ek jeeb k neeche.
Aur shuru zubaan pe musical chair ka khel.
Neeche waali ko upar aur upar waali ko neeche.
Kabhi clockwise toh kabhi anti clockwise.
BEAT
Slow motion. Uncle, eyes closed. Savouring the Tic Tac. Face completely at peace. Lost in it. The metro, the crowd, the phone — all gone.
CLOSE
He finishes the round. Three Tic Tacs left in the box.
3 goli left in the box. Come on uncle, you can do it!
01
The Zag
Every Tic Tac ad targets youth. The uncle format is the opposite of what the category expects. The contrast between a tough-looking 50-year-old with a moustache and a young playful voiceover is where the comedy lives.
02
The Irony
Uncles tell youth to stop using phones. Now uncles are the ones most stuck in the scroll loop. The voiceover preaching to the uncle does what the uncle used to do to everyone else. Recognition without cruelty.
03
The Slow Mo Moment
The uncle savouring the Tic Tac with his eyes closed is the visual that earns everything. He is not doing something youth-coded. He is doing something better. Fully present, completely absorbed. The product delivers what the phone couldn't.
Tic Tac.

04 / UPI Lite
UPI Lite — TVC Concept

Lite Karo.
Enlite Raho.

The weight of every small transaction. And the relief of letting it go.

Absurdist TVC Fintech Visual Comedy
The Insight
"What if your bank statement had a face? And that face was yours, ageing in real time?"

UPI Lite removes small transactions from your bank statement. The functional benefit is obvious. The emotional benefit was underserved.

The insight: a cluttered bank statement creates a psychological heaviness disproportionate to the size of individual transactions. Rs. 6 for chai. Rs. 18 for parking. None of it matters. All of it accumulates. The product does not just clean your statement. It cleans your relationship with money.

Setting the ad in a meditation centre makes the metaphor physical. Enlightenment and Enlitement become the same word. The wordplay earns itself because the setting earns it first.

Four Scenes
Scene 01
The Weight
Meditation centre. A man trying to concentrate. Bank notification sounds hit one after another. Rs. 1 lakh, Rs. 18, Rs. 340, Rs. 6. With each, his face tightens. Wrinkles form. He begins ageing. His bank statement appears on his forehead. His head flattens and widens, literally becoming a statement.
Scene 02
The Lift
The man next to him. Same state, same frustration. Then a sound. UPI Lite downloaded. Instantly he lifts off the ground, cross-legged, floating. Wrinkles fade. Statement slides off his face onto the mat below. Clean. Only: salary, rent, UPI Lite top-up. He is smiling.
Scene 03
The Ashram
Every phone rings at once. Download complete. UPI Lite. One by one, every person in the ashram begins to float, cross-legged above their mats. Statements clean below them. Faces light. The room fills upward.
Scene 04
The Last Man
Our first man. Still on the ground. Still frowning. Head still a bank statement. He looks around. Everyone is floating. His phone buzzes. Download complete. He looks down. Looks up. Taps. He rises.
01
The Visual Idea
The head becoming a bank statement is the kind of image that, once seen, explains the product without a word of copy. It does not describe the benefit. It shows what the problem feels like from the inside. That is the test of a visual idea.
02
Why the Ashram
Placing a fintech ad in an ashram does two things: it creates unexpected contrast between money and meditation, and it earns the wordplay in the tagline. Enlite is not a pun. It is the point. The setting makes it land honestly.
03
The Last Beat
Saving the first man for last turns the ad into a story with a shape. He is the viewer. His hesitation is the hesitation. His rising is the download. The audience has already wanted him to rise for thirty seconds by the time he does.
Lite Karo. Enlite Raho.
UPI Lite
05 / Roff
Roff Tile Adhesives — TVC Concept

Cementia
Jugaad

The labour knows what works. The contractor just needed to be told.

TVC Construction Humour
The Insight
"Cement became the default not because it was right, but because nobody had offered an alternative. The labour knew this before anyone else."

The product pitch for Roff is incomplete without two characters: the labour and the contractor. The contractor could be shown as corrupt or as unaware. Showing him as corrupt makes it allegorical and alienates a decision-maker. Showing him as simply unaware gives him a benefit of the doubt that makes the ad warmer and more honest.

The labour is the real hero because he is the actual end user in the entire industry. He knows what works from experience. His approval carries more weight than any celebrity or expert because it comes from someone who has lived the problem. He is the friend who always pitches the right product wherever he goes.

The aata, chawal, basmati analogy is the creative device that makes the product truth funny without making the contractor look stupid. He is not wrong to be confused. Nobody had explained it to him this way before.

The Script
OPEN
Contractor on phone: "Tile chipkane k liye cement bhijwa do."
BEAT 1
Labour steps forward, stops him. "Sir, rukiye." Contractor confused, covers phone. "Kya hua?"
BEAT 2
"Cement se tile chipkayenge sir, daliye sey nahi?" Contractor: "KYA?" Labour: "Accha chalo batao aata kaisa rahega?" Contractor, annoyed: "Pagal ho gaye ho kya?"
BEAT 3
"Acha aata nahi, fir geele chawal, basmati kaisa rahega?" Contractor: "Band karo ye bakwas."
RESOLVE
Labour, pause, looks at him: "Sir, agar formality hi karni ho toh ye options bhi utney hi juggadu hai jitna ki cement." Contractor: "Tum batao fir, cement se mazboot kya hai?" Labour: "Magar ki jakad sir. Pidilite Roff." Contractor, back on phone: "Cancel karo cement. Roff bhijwa do."
01
Why the Labour is the Hero
The labour is the actual end user of the product. His hands do the work. His reputation depends on the result. When he recommends Roff it carries the weight of someone who has lived the difference between cement and the right material. No celebrity can match that credibility.
02
The Analogy Device
Aata, chawal, basmati used as analogies for cement makes the product truth funny without making the contractor look corrupt or stupid. He used cement because nobody had offered him anything else. The analogy is the moment of clarity, not the moment of humiliation.
03
The Villain
The villain is not cement and not the contractor. The villain is the absence of a better option for so long that cement became the default. Roff does not beat cement. It fills the gap that cement was never meant to fill.
Tile ke liye sirf Roff. Koi Cementia jugaad nahi.

06 / Colgate
Colgate — TVC Concept

Jhugaali

The buffalo doesn't care. You should.

TVC Night Brushing Observational Comedy
The Insight
"Every toothpaste ad shows the habit in place. This one shows what happens while you sleep when you don't have it."

Most night brushing communication tells you about bacteria, cavities, and consequences. The audience already knows. They just don't act. The problem is not awareness. It is the moment when sleep wins over the effort of brushing.

The real insight is biological. Eight hours of reduced saliva flow means bacteria feed on leftover food particles all night. The body does what the buffalo does — jhugaali — without you knowing. The buffalo has an excuse. It is biologically designed for this. You are not.

The distinction is not mean. It is honest. The buffalo is not the villain. He is simply unbothered. That unbothered quality is what makes the comparison funny rather than judgmental.

The Script
SCENE 1
Quiet bedroom. Night. Soft moonlight. A man sleeping. Happy face. Mouth slightly moving, savouring the palak stuck in his teeth and chewing it a little.
SCENE 2
A buffalo. Closed eyes. Same energy. Calm. Unbothered. Chewing slowly. Green paalak visible. Does not care about anything around him.
VOICEOVER
"Raat mein jhugaali sirf bhains ko suit karti hai, insaano ko nahi."
CLOSE
Night scene at a sink. Colgate. Buffalo still chewing somewhere in the background. Unbothered. Crickets. Night insects.
01
The Visual Parallel
Man sleeping and buffalo chewing are shown side by side as mirror images. No explanation needed. The audience closes the loop themselves. The parallel is uncomfortable enough to be memorable and absurd enough to be funny.
02
The Buffalo's Role
The buffalo is not a villain. He is genuinely unbothered because he has no reason to care. He is biologically built for this. The ad does not mock him. His indifference is the joke, not the punchline. This keeps the tone warm rather than cruel.
03
Sound Off Test
The entire ad works without sound. Man chewing in sleep, buffalo chewing slowly, sink with Colgate. The voiceover adds the distinction. The visual does the argument. This is the test of whether an idea is visual or just verbal.
Raat ko brush karo.

07 / Colgate
Colgate — TVC Concept

Acchi
Aadate

A mother's quiet act of love that travels further than she knows.

TVC Night Brushing Emotional
The Insight
"Every toothpaste ad shows the habit already in place. This one shows the moment it almost breaks, and what brings it back."

The brief was to convince people to brush at night. The problem is not awareness. Everyone knows they should. The problem is the first night away from home, the first time no one is watching, the first time the bed wins before the bathroom.

The mother does not hand the toothpaste to her daughter. She packs it quietly while the daughter is looking elsewhere. The daughter does not see it happen. The mother prepares for a moment the daughter does not even know is coming.

That is not a product placement. That is love made practical. The toothpaste rolling out of the bag is not a brand moment. It is the mother arriving at the exact right time, the way mothers do.

The Script
SCENE 1
Bus stop. A girl around 14, leaving for hostel for the first time from a small town. Not talking much. Lost in emotions and nervousness. Mother enters hastily, hands her a box of food, and slips something into the bag from behind. Zip closes quietly.
"Hostel jaakar achi aadate mat bhul jaana."
She boards the bus.
SCENE 2
Hostel. First night. Everything alien. She settles in the room. Two beds. She takes the empty one. No roommate yet. Slides luggage under the bed, keeps the backpack. Tired. Missing home. All set to sleep when the bag under her head falls and the half-zipped chain lets the Colgate roll onto the floor. She stares at it. Her mother's voice, in her memory:
"Hostel jaakar achi aadate mat bhul jaana."
She picks the paste. Heads to the washroom.
SCENE 3
Bathroom. Her roommate already at the mirror, brushing with the same Colgate tube. Her clothes show she is from a city. Same first hostel night written on her face. They see each other in the mirror. Smile. Exhausted. Relieved. Two girls. Two mothers. The same quiet act of love, packed into a bag without making a show of it.
01
Why Silent Packing
The mother packs the toothpaste while the daughter is looking at the platform. The daughter never sees it go in. This small detail makes the discovery in the hostel room a delayed gift rather than an instruction. The gap between the giving and the receiving is where the emotion lives.
02
The Effort Beat
Getting up from the bed is the hardest action in the ad. It is shown honestly. Not graceful, not inspirational. Just real. The audience has been in that exact moment. Showing it honestly is more convincing than any dental statistic.
03
The Second Girl
Two girls from different backgrounds, same toothpaste, same first night. The ad stops being one girl's story. It becomes every mother's story. Two mothers who never met prepared for the same moment without knowing the other existed. Colgate is the common thread.
Jagah nayee. Aadat wahi.

08 / Campaign Breakdowns

Reading
What Already Exists

Three brands. Three different relationships with their audiences. Three different outcomes when that relationship was tested.

Amul
Observational commentary since 1967. The Amul Girl.
60 Years. Still Relevant.

Amul's style of communication has been consistent across three generations. Governments have changed, economic policy has changed, cultures have changed, and yet the Amul girl speaks to every common man and woman through topical commentary on global issues, national issues, and pop culture. It creates a community and it is constant in doing so.

What Amul cracked is the tone. It is not the tone of a brand instructing its audience. It is the tone of the common Indian citizen observing life. That distinction is everything. Sixty years of maintaining that tone without slipping into preaching, without overreaching into territory that would alienate any part of its audience, is the real achievement.

It creates relatability in the short run and cultural relevance in the longer run. The topical commentary is the format. The underlying promise is: we see what you see, and we will always show up to notice it with you.

The brand that lasts is not the one with the best product. It is the one that becomes part of how people make sense of what is happening around them.
Gillette
We Believe: The Best Men Can Be. MeToo era TVC.
Miscommunication

Gillette had thirty years of "The Best a Man Can Get." That line built loyalty among men. The brand stood beside men. It patronised them in the best sense of the word. When a man used Gillette he was told he deserved the best. That is a relationship built on respect and aspiration.

The MeToo era TVC showed men as cruel, misogynist, and violent toward women. Though this was a social fact and there was nothing wrong in what was being depicted, it came from a brand that had spent thirty years standing beside those same men. When it comes from such a brand, the TVC turned into an allegation. It felt like backstabbing.

The intent was to shed toxic behaviour the way a razor sheds a beard. The metaphor is clean. But the execution broke the established relationship without renegotiating it first. The audience felt accused by someone who was supposed to know them. That is not a creative failure. It is a trust failure.

A brand can only shift its relationship with its audience after it has given that audience a reason to follow it there. Gillette moved without asking anyone to come along.
Tanishq
Ekatvam. 2020. Withdrawn under political pressure.
Strategic Failure

The Ekatvam campaign was not a creative flaw. The cause was right. The audience backed it. Progressive urban women who had always stood with Tanishq's previous stands were behind this film entirely. The creative read the room correctly.

The failure was strategic. When organised political pressure came, Tanishq withdrew the campaign. In doing so, it abandoned the very customers who had stood with it at the moment of pressure. The withdrawal signalled that the brand's commitment to its audience was conditional. That broke the relationship from the other side.

The lesson is not to avoid cultural stands. It is to only take a stand when you are prepared to defend it. A brand that retreats at the first challenge tells its audience that it does not actually believe what it said. That is a more damaging message than any campaign could be.

Before taking a stand, the only question that matters is not whether your audience agrees. It is whether you are prepared to stay when they need you to.
