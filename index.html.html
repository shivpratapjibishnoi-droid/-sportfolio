About

I've been noticing things my whole life without knowing there was a name for it. These three stories are the most honest answer I have to why that name might be advertising.

In 8th standard, I borrowed money from a friend and bought a Cadbury Silk for a girl. I wasn't really thinking about chocolate.

I was following a script that Cadbury had already written — that in India, if you love someone, you give them a Silk. The ad had done its job so completely that I wasn't even aware it had done anything.

I was just a boy with borrowed money, standing outside a classroom, holding purple packaging like it was a marriage proposal. I was still in 8th standard. What's love for that teen? What the girl said is a separate story and not the point.

That's when I realised tapping the right emotion is the backbone of any narrative.

"Darr ke aage jeet hai" was my wallpaper throughout UPSC preparation. Not because I'm a Mountain Dew loyalist but because fear is the one emotion that nobody escapes.

Gabbar Singh knew it. Field Marshal Manekshaw once said that anyone who claims he feels no fear is either lying or he's a Gorkha. This was his way of saying fear is the most honest human condition there is.

Mountain Dew just found a way to sell a drink through it. That's what I find remarkable about advertising. Someone sat in a room, picked fear out of the thousands of human emotions available, and built something that outlasted the campaign. Nike did it with laziness. Cadbury did it with love. The product is almost incidental.

While conducting field research for a Bangalore-based political consultancy during the 2024 Lok Sabha elections, I realised the same behaviour applies to voters.

Choosing a candidate is not very different from picking a packet of chips off a rack. Every option has a USP, but only the one that taps the right emotion gets picked. The mechanics are the same be it Mountain Dew or Cadbury, just a different product.

Still finding those connections, only this time where it belongs the most.
